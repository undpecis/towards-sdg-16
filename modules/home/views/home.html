In the Europe and the Commonwealth of Independent States (ECIS) region, the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP) works with local, national and international partners to strengthen inclusive and effective governance and peace, by advocating, advising, enabling discussion, fostering consensus and capacitating institutions. UNDP achieves these goals by creating an environment in which all partners, including civil society, can grow in strength and contribute towards sustainable development.

  • Background//mobile//

    An ethnically diverse nation with large oil, gas and mineral reserves, Kazakhstan has experienced rapid economic growth since the turn of the century and has become the dominant economy in Central Asia. However, with oil and other petroleum products making up over 60 percent of exports, the fall in prices for many commodities in 2015 had a significant impact on the Kazakh economy.

    In April 2015, Nursultan Nazarbaev won the latest presidential election with a reported 97.7 percent of the vote.59 His re-election coincided with the launch of an important reform process, entitled ‘100 Concrete Steps to Implement Five Institutional Reforms’, which is anticipated to strongly impact on institutional efficiency, government openness and long-term economic development. Furthermore, the President has made a commitment to reforms encouraging a multi-party system, while the country has also been exploring ways to decentralize power to the local level, with several initiatives from the Government in this area. To date, however, there is still significant potential for the further decentralization of power to local executive bodies.

    Over the past two decades, Kazakhstan has implemented significant public administration and civil service reforms to improve the effectiveness and efficiency of government. Nonetheless, more progress is needed in addressing the development challenges facing Kazakhstan. Restrictions on freedom of expression remain, with newspapers and other media outlets presenting dissenting views still subject to closure by authorities. Further improvement is also required to reduce discrimination against ethnic minorities, the LGTBI community and some religious groups.

    More recently, Kazakhstan has been playing an active role in attempts to counter violent extremism in Central Asia. In June 2015, Kazakhstan hosted the two-day Central and South Asia Regional Conference on Countering Violent Extremism. Co-organized by the Kazakh and US governments, the event was attended by 200 high-level government officials, representatives of the private sector and civil society activists from Afghanistan, Bangladesh, Kazakhstan, the Kyrgyz Republic, India, Pakistan, Sri Lanka, Tajikistan and Uzbekistan. As a result of the conference, a set of recommendations were adopted and concrete steps proposed to enhance national and regional efforts.

Regional context

The ECIS region is a geographically, economically and politically diverse region. While all countries and territories in the region (except Albania and Turkey) were part of the former Soviet Union or the former Yugoslavia, they have diverged significantly from that shared past. The establishment of democratic institutions, market economies and conflict prevention strategies have had different levels of success. Foreign policy decisions to prioritise a relationship with the Russian Federation, the European Union (EU), or neither, have also led to very different approaches to governance and domestic policy.

Economically, all countries and territories in the region are considered middle income, however, significant variation exists within this categorization. In 2014, gross domestic product (GDP) per capita varied from over US$10,000 in Turkey to just over US$1,000 in Tajikistan. This variation in GDP also alludes to some of the different drivers of these economies. Countries such as Azerbaijan, Kazakhstan and Turkmenistan have undergone rapid economic growth as a result of large resource endowments and high commodity prices. Other countries, such as the Kyrgyz Republic, Tajikistan, and Moldova, have seen slower growth and remain heavily reliant on agriculture and remittances from migrant workers.

The different political and economic environments in the region provide not only a range of challenges in themselves, but also impact on how different events and crises are experienced. In order to be effective, UNDP’s governance and peacebuilding support intrinsically takes into account how socio-economic and political developments impact each country and territory in the region, and in turn affect UNDP’s programming.
